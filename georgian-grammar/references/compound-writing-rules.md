# Compound Writing Rules

Use this file for joined writing, separate writing, and hyphenation.

## Decision Steps

1. Decide whether the expression is one lexical word or a syntactic phrase.
2. Before applying a productive rule, ask whether the whole form is an established lexicalized word or fixed adverbial expression.
3. Check whether both parts still keep independent grammatical force.
4. If the structure is `word + word = compound` and the underlying relation can be expanded with `·Éì·Éê`, use a hyphen between the components only when the standard form keeps the coordinate pairing visible.
5. Use a hyphen only when the parts form one compound but retain coordinate value, or when a specific orthographic rule requires it.
6. If the issue is a fixed particle or discourse expression, also read `particle-expression-rules.md`.

## Source-Backed Hyphen Rule

When two words are compressed into one compound and the relation between them is equivalent to coordination with `·Éì·Éê`, write the components with a hyphen:

- noun + noun
- adjective + adjective
- numeral + numeral
- paired actions or paired time/location words

This rule applies to compound formation. It does not mean every phrase containing `·Éì·Éê` should be hyphenated.

## Lexicalized Override

Do not let a productive hyphen or separate-writing rule override an established single lexical item.

Use joined writing when the whole expression behaves as one standard word, even if its historical parts look coordinate, poetic, or transparent. This is a lexical-norm boundary, not a memorization shortcut: if the local rules do not settle the form, route to `lexical-norm-boundary.md`.

Useful warning signs:

- the whole form names one concept rather than two equal members
- the expression functions as a fixed adverbial form
- the components no longer accept normal phrase-level modification
- a school-style prompt contrasts joined, separate, and hyphenated variants of the same expression

## Joined Writing

Write together when the whole form functions as one lexicalized adverb, pronoun, or compound and the parts no longer behave as a normal phrase.

Useful patterns:

- repeated or paired stems that form one adverbial unit: `·Éì·ÉÝ·Éù·Éì·Éê·Éì·ÉÝ·Éù`, `·Éí·Éñ·Éê·Éì·Éê·Éí·Éñ·Éê`
- fixed pronoun/adverbial compounds: `·É°·ÉÆ·Éï·Éê·Éì·Éê·É°·ÉÆ·Éï·Éê`, `·ÉØ·Éî·ÉÝ·ÉØ·Éî·ÉÝ·Éù·Éë·Éò·Éó`
- lexicalized particles where the whole form is fixed: `·Éõ·Éê·Éò·Éú·É™·Éì·Éê·Éõ·Éê·Éò·Éú·É™`

These examples illustrate patterns; they are not a replacement for the rule.

## Separate Writing

Write separately when the expression remains a phrase with independent words or a transparent syntactic relation.

Useful patterns:

- interrogative or pronoun plus verbal noun plus modal: `·ÉÝ·Éê ·Éó·É•·Éõ·Éê ·É£·Éú·Éì·Éê`
- emphatic repetition with `·Éì·Éê` where both sides remain words: `·Éõ·ÉÆ·Éù·Éö·Éù·Éì ·Éì·Éê ·Éõ·ÉÆ·Éù·Éö·Éù·Éì`
- transparent phrase-like repetition: `·Éë·Éù·Éö·Éù·É° ·Éì·Éê ·Éë·Éù·Éö·Éù·É°`

Do not collapse a phrase into one word only because it is frequent.

## Hyphenation

Use a hyphen for word-internal connection, not for clause separation.

High-value cases:

- measurement plus `·Éú·Éê·ÉÆ·Éî·Éï·Éê·ÉÝ·Éò` after a formed measurement expression: `·Éù·ÉÝ·Éô·Éò·Éö·Éù-·Éú·Éê·ÉÆ·Éî·Éï·Éê·ÉÝ·Éò`
- numeral or bound first component plus `·Éú·Éê·ÉÆ·Éî·Éï·Éê·ÉÝ·Éò` in strict school-style spelling: `·Éî·ÉÝ·Éó-·Éú·Éê·ÉÆ·Éî·Éï·Éê·ÉÝ·Éò`, `·Éú·Éê·ÉÆ·Éî·Éï·Éê·ÉÝ-·É§·Éò·Éú·Éê·Éö·Éò`
- coordinate compounds where the standard form keeps both components visibly paired

Do not use a hyphen for ordinary syntactic phrases, and do not use a dash where a hyphen is needed.

## Fast Check

If the expression answers "what single lexical item is this?" joined writing may be possible. If it answers "what phrase is this?" separate writing is safer. If it joins two equal word components inside one lexical item, consider a hyphen and verify the local rule.

# Polite Forms and Punctuation

## Polite Address

- Use `თქვენ` with plural verbal morphology.
  - `თქვენ ხართ`
  - `თქვენ იცით`
  - `თქვენ გინდათ`
  - `თქვენ შეგიძლიათ`
- For direct address, use `ბატონო` or `ქალბატონო` plus the name.
  - `ბატონო გიორგი`
  - `ქალბატონო ნინო`
- Use `ბატონი` or `ქალბატონი` for respectful third-person reference.
  - `ქალბატონი მარიამი მოგვიანებით მოვა`

## Common Polite Substitutions

Use these only when the register is genuinely formal.

- `არის` -> `გახლავთ`, `ბრძანდება`
- `ხარ` -> `ბრძანდებით`
- `დაჯექი` -> `დაბრძანდით`
- `შედი` -> `შემობრძანდით`
- `თქვი` -> `ბრძანეთ`
- `გსურს` -> `გნებავთ`
- `აიღე` -> `ინებეთ`

Do not overuse these forms in neutral informational prose. Formality should match the communicative situation.

## Punctuation

- Let syntax drive punctuation; do not place commas by English rhythm alone.
- Do not put a space before `, ; : ? !`.
- Usually keep one space after those marks unless a closing quote or bracket follows.
- Before choosing a punctuation mark, classify what the mark is doing: separating clauses, separating homogeneous members, introducing a list, marking a summary, isolating direct address, isolating an inserted phrase, or marking direct speech.
- Distinguish hyphen from dash:
  - hyphen joins parts of a lexical unit
  - dash separates clauses, introduces direct speech, or marks insertion
- In multi-clause sentences, verify where the clause boundaries really are before finalizing commas.
- Use commas around detached or inserted modifiers and parenthetical material.
- In direct speech, use a dash consistently rather than a plain hyphen.

### Commas

- Use commas to separate clause boundaries when two finite predicates or a subordinate clause create separate syntactic parts.
  - `ვიცი, რომ მოხვალ.`
- Use commas around direct address.
  - `მეგობრებო, დღეს დავიწყოთ.`
  - `დღეს, მეგობრებო, დავიწყოთ.`
- Use commas around inserted or parenthetical words and phrases.
  - `ეს, რა თქმა უნდა, მნიშვნელოვანია.`
- Use commas between homogeneous members of a list unless the final pair is joined by a single non-repeated `და`.
  - `ვიყიდე პური, ყველი და ხილი.`
  - `ვიყიდე პური და ყველი.`
- With repeated conjunctions, check whether each repeated part is a separate homogeneous member and usually separate them with commas.
  - `მოვიდა ნინოც, გიორგიც და მარიც.`

### Enumerations and Generalizing Words

- If a generalizing word or clause comes before a list that specifies it, use a colon before the list.
  - `ყველაფერი წარსულში დარჩა: სილამაზე, სილაღე და ახალგაზრდობა.`
  - Here `სილამაზე, სილაღე და ახალგაზრდობა` specifies `ყველაფერი`; do not choose a dash in this direction.
- If the list comes first and the summarizing/generalizing word or clause follows, use a dash before the summary.
  - `სილამაზე, სილაღე და ახალგაზრდობა — ყველაფერი წარსულში დარჩა.`
- In multiple-choice punctuation tasks, first decide whether the list explains a previous generalizing element or is later summarized by one; then select colon vs dash from that direction.

### Colon, Dash, and Semicolon

- Use a colon after an announcing or generalizing part when the following words disclose, specify, or enumerate it.
  - `ერთ რამეს გთხოვ: დროულად მოდი.`
- Use a dash for a following summary, abrupt consequence, contrast, or inserted explanatory segment when the syntax calls for separation rather than list introduction.
  - `ყველა მოვიდა — შეხვედრა დაიწყო.`
- Use a semicolon only when comma-separated parts are too long or already contain internal commas and stronger separation is needed.
  - `ერთ მხარეს იდგნენ მოსწავლეები, მშობლები და მასწავლებლები; მეორე მხარეს — სტუმრები.`
- Do not choose between colon and dash by visual habit. Ask whether the second part is a list/explanation of what came before, or whether the second part summarizes what came before.

### Inserted And Non-Member Words

Some words and phrases do not function as ordinary sentence members: direct address, interjection, parenthetical phrase, source marker, evaluative/modal word, or speaker attitude marker.

- First ask whether the word answers a sentence-member question. If no clear question applies, test whether it comments on the whole statement.
- Use commas around parenthetical or non-member material when it interrupts or frames the main sentence.
- Common functions include certainty, limitation, source, contrast, transition, emotional reaction, or speaker evaluation.
- For sentence-initial evaluative adverbs, distinguish ordinary manner/time meaning from modal use. If it evaluates the whole proposition, comma separation is likely in school-style punctuation.
- Do not leave a space before a comma even if the source or quiz option displays one.

### Direct Speech

- For dialogue lines, use a dash before the speaker's words.
  - `- სად მიდიხარ?`
- Keep author words and speech punctuation consistent; do not replace the dash with a hyphen.

## Quick Checks

- If you see `თქვენ` plus a singular verb, fix it first.
- If you see `ბატონი გიორგი,` in direct address, convert it to `ბატონო გიორგი,`.
- If you see spaces before commas or question marks, remove them.
- If a list specifies a previous word such as `ყველაფერი`, `ყველა`, `ეს`, or `შემდეგი`, check for a missing colon before the list.
- If a sentence has several predicates, mark clause boundaries before deciding comma placement.
- If an opening word is evaluative or parenthetical rather than a true adverbial member, check for comma separation.
- If the punctuation mark is `:` or `—`, verify whether it introduces a list/explanation or marks a summary/consequence.
- If a sentence feels too English in its punctuation, simplify the clause structure and repunctuate from syntax.

# Verb Morphology Rules

Use this file when checking Georgian verb forms, especially when two forms both sound possible.

## Decision Steps

1. Identify the intended meaning and participants: who acts, who receives, and whether the verb is transitive, intransitive, medial, or indirect-object-taking.
2. Decide whether the form is primary or noun-derived. A noun-derived verb must still follow a real Georgian verb pattern.
3. Identify the series or tense/mood before judging the surface form.
4. Split the form into preverb or lexical prefix, version vowel, person markers, root, theme suffix, and ending where possible.
5. Do not create a present-tense form by simply attaching `-ობ` to a noun or verbal noun. First check whether the verb belongs to an `-ობ` theme class.
6. Prefer the standard literary paradigm over an analogical colloquial form in grammar questions and polished writing.

## Source-Backed Controls

Georgian verb analysis must account for:

- primary versus noun-derived verbs
- grammatical person and number
- subjective and objective person markers
- one-person, two-person, and three-person verb structures
- screeves and series
- changing stems across screeves
- theme signs: `ი`, `ავ`, `ამ`, `ებ`, `ობ`, and rarer `ემ`/`ო`
- active, passive, benefactive, reflexive, and causative patterns
- third-series inversion

Use these as the checklist before accepting a form that only sounds plausible.

## Person Marking

- First-person subject often appears as `ვ-`; first-person object appears as `მ-` or `გვ-`.
- Second-person object appears as `გ-`; plural marking is handled by `-თ` where the paradigm requires it.
- Object markers can appear inside the verb complex; do not move them before lexical prefixes by surface analogy.
- With lexical prefixes such as `თანა-`, preserve the lexical prefix before the personal complex:
  - standard: `თანაგიგრძნობ`
  - standard: `თანავუგრძნობ`
  - avoid analogical ordering such as `გითანაგრძნობ`

## Theme Suffix Caution

The suffix `-ობ` is valid for many verbs, but it is not a universal way to make a verb from a noun. If a candidate looks like `ვ-` + noun/root + `-ობ`, check whether the verb's standard present stem actually uses that class.

For strict standard usage, do not accept an analogical `-ობ` form just because it is understandable in conversation. Derive from the verb's paradigm.

If a disputed form differs only because one option was made by analogy with a noun or verbal noun, choose the paradigm-derived literary form. The reason is not a memorized spelling trap; the form must be derived from the standard verb paradigm.

If the local rules do not establish which stem belongs to the strict literary paradigm, treat the question as a lexical-norm case and use `lexical-norm-boundary.md`.

Theme signs `-ავ` and `-ამ` are not interchangeable. Some verbal nouns and roots predict one pattern in perfect or present forms, but many concrete verbs must be checked by paradigm. If the choice is exactly `-ავ` versus `-ამ` and the local rule does not settle the verb, route it as a lexical-norm case instead of guessing from a nearby form.

## Voice, Benefactive, And Causative Patterns

Before translating or correcting a verb, identify whether the action is neutral, for oneself, for someone else, passive, or caused/helped by another participant.

- Passive forms can move the active object into subject position; do not preserve the English active structure automatically.
- `ი-` often marks reflexive or self-benefactive action; `უ-` often marks action done for another participant; unmarked or `ა-` forms can be neutral. Concrete verbs still need paradigm checking.
- Causative or helping constructions often use `-ინ-` or `-ევინ-` patterns, as in "make/help someone do X". Do not replace them with the simple active form when another participant causes or enables the action.
- If a form differs only by version vowel or causative marker, identify the participant roles first: actor, beneficiary, affected person, and object.

## Carrying Versus Leading

For movement/transport verbs, choose the paradigm from the kind of moved object:

- use `მიაქვს`/`მოაქვს` type forms for things being carried or brought
- use `მიჰყავს`/`მოჰყავს` type forms for people, animals, or vehicles being led/driven/taken

Do not translate English "take/bring" with one Georgian verb. First decide whether the object is a carried thing or a led/driven participant.

## O-Final Noun-Derived Verbs

For verbs derived from nominal bases that end in `-ო`, do not decide the form by surface sound, by a single remembered screeve, or by the broad label "second series". First identify the concrete screeve and its function.

- In present and future-group forms, do not insert a stem `-ვ-` merely because the base ends in `-ო`.
- In aorist/წყვეტილი forms that express a completed action, this class can use an `-ოვ-` stem.
- Do not carry the aorist `-ოვ-` stem automatically into second subjunctive/optative or prohibitive forms. The second subjunctive/optative must be checked as its own screeve, even though it is traditionally grouped with the second series.
- In first-person forms, separate the subject marker `ვ-` from the stem alternant `-ვ-`. The subject marker appears near the front of the verb complex; the stem `-ვ-` appears near the base before the ending. Do not count one as the other or move it to the wrong position.
- If a candidate differs only by presence, absence, or position of this `-ვ-`, split the form into preverb, person marker, version vowel, base, series stem, and ending before answering.
- If the local rule does not settle whether a concrete verb belongs to this class, treat it as a lexical-norm case and verify the paradigm externally.

## Version And Vowel Boundaries

Repeated vowels can be real morpheme boundaries. Before deleting or adding a vowel, split the form:

- preverb
- version vowel
- root-initial vowel
- theme suffix or ending

If two vowels meet at a true boundary, preserve both.

## Third-Series Inversion

Third-series transitive verbs often invert the expected subject/object marking. Recheck who is the logical subject and which participant controls the agreement before finalizing forms like `დამიწერია`, `მინახავს`, or `მიკეთებია`.

## Evidential Past With `თურმე`

For a past action known from someone else's words or inferred from the result rather than witnessed directly, Georgian often uses a perfect/third-series-style form with `თურმე`.

- Recheck inversion and person marking before forming the verb.
- Do not let `თურმე` alone carry the grammar; the verb form must also match the evidential/result-state construction.
- If the exact perfect stem depends on the verb's lexical paradigm, route through `lexical-norm-boundary.md`.

## Fast Standardness Check

For a doubtful verb form, ask:

1. Is this a paradigm form or a noun-derived analogy?
2. Are the person and object markers in the right place?
3. Is a version vowel required?
4. Is the `-ობ` suffix part of the verb class or only copied from a noun?
5. Is this a neutral, self-benefactive, other-benefactive, passive, or causative construction?
6. If the form is derived from an `-ო`-final base, have I identified the series and separated subject `ვ-` from stem `-ვ-`?
7. Is the form literary, colloquial, dialectal, or archaic?

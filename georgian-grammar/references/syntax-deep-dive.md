# Syntax Deep Dive

Use this file only for complex syntax: multi-clause sentences, subordination, conditionals, indirect speech, relative clauses, long punctuation questions, or Georgian naturalness problems caused by English/Russian calque. For simple agreement or punctuation checks, use the focused reference files first.

This file is a syntax decision guide. It should help choose structure, not memorize model sentences.

## Syntax Triage

Before editing, decide what kind of syntax problem is present:

- agreement between subject and predicate
- case or postposition governance
- main clause plus subordinate clause
- relative clause
- real or unreal condition
- cause, purpose, result, comparison, or contrast
- direct speech, indirect speech, or reported-speech marker
- overloaded word order or translation calque
- punctuation caused by clause structure

If the issue is actually morphology or lexical paradigm, return to the morphology references.

## Sentence Skeleton

For a difficult sentence, strip it to the skeleton:

1. Mark every finite predicate.
2. Identify the main clause.
3. Identify dependent clauses and their linkers.
4. Identify each clause's subject, object, indirect object, and modifier.
5. Check whether a modifier belongs to the nearest noun, the whole clause, or an inserted phrase.

Do not place commas or choose linkers before the clause structure is clear.

## Clause Relation Route

Choose the linker from the relation:

- cause: `რადგან`, `ვინაიდან`, `იმიტომ, რომ`
- purpose: `რათა`, `იმისთვის, რომ`, infinitive/adverbial-purpose constructions such as `სასწავლებლად`
- real time: `როცა`, `როდესაც`, `როგორც კი`
- real condition: `თუ`, `რომ`, `თუ ... მაშინ ...`
- unreal or wished-for condition: `რომ` with counterfactual or desired-state forms
- contrast: `მაგრამ`, contrastive `კი`, or a rewritten contrast structure
- explanation or specification: `ანუ`, `ესე იგი`, or a colon/dash structure if punctuation is the issue

Do not choose a conjunction by translating English "that", "if", "when", or "so that" alone.

## Conditionals

For conditionals, identify the reality status:

- Real condition/result: the condition can happen and the result follows. Use `თუ`, `რომ`, `როცა/როდესაც`, or `როგორც კი` as the context requires.
- Unreal past condition: the condition did not happen; the result also did not happen. Use a counterfactual `რომ` construction and a conditional result.
- Unreal present/future or wished-for condition: the condition is currently false or unlikely; use `რომ` with the suitable desired-state/counterfactual form.
- Request or bargain nuance: use `ოღონდ` only when the meaning is "provided that / just do this".

If the sentence mixes real and unreal frames, fix the verb forms before punctuation.

## Relative Clauses

For `რომელიც`, `ვინც`, and `რაც`:

- Use `რომელიც` when the relative pronoun modifies a concrete antecedent.
- Choose `რომელიც/რომლებიც` by the number of referents.
- Choose the case of `რომელიც` by its role inside the relative clause.
- Use `ვინც` for a person or actor.
- Use `რაც` for content, event, thing, action-object, or "what/that which" relations.

Do not copy the antecedent's visible case automatically. A dative antecedent can still require an ergative relative form inside the relative clause, and the reverse can also happen.

## Direct And Indirect Speech

Separate three tasks:

1. Direct quotation: preserve the utterance and punctuate speech consistently.
2. Indirect speech: adjust person, perspective, and sometimes tense or mood.
3. Reported-speech suffix: choose `-მეთქი`, `-თქო`, or `-ო` by speaker perspective.

Perspective controls the suffix:

- `-მეთქი`: I repeat my own earlier words or thought.
- `-თქო`: I tell someone to pass on my words.
- `-ო`: I report someone else's words.

When converting direct to indirect speech, do not leave first-person and second-person pronouns unchanged if the speaker perspective has changed.

## Word Order And Natural Georgian

Georgian word order is flexible, but it is not random. Use word order to support topic, focus, and clarity:

- Keep tightly connected modifiers near the noun they modify.
- Avoid copying long English noun chains; split them or convert them into Georgian postpositional or relative structures.
- Avoid stacking several subordinate clauses before the reader knows the main predicate.
- Prefer clear Georgian verbs over literal calques with weak helper verbs.
- In drafting mode, rewrite a sentence if the grammatical version is correct but still sounds translated.

For public-facing prose, naturalness is part of correctness.

## Syntax-Driven Punctuation

Punctuation follows syntax:

- Put commas at real clause boundaries, around direct address, and around inserted or parenthetical material.
- Use commas between homogeneous members unless a single non-repeated `და` joins the final pair.
- Use a colon when a following list or explanation specifies a previous generalizing word or clause.
- Use a dash when a later element summarizes, contrasts, or adds an explanatory break.
- Keep direct speech dash usage consistent; do not replace it with a hyphen.

If the punctuation choice is unclear, redraw the sentence skeleton and identify whether the second part explains what came before or summarizes what came before.

## Ambiguity And Safe Rewriting

If syntax allows two readings:

1. Name the ambiguity internally: owner, subject, referent, condition, or clause boundary.
2. Prefer an explicit Georgian rewrite over a risky compact sentence.
3. Repeat the noun or owner when `თავისი/მისი`, `რომელიც`, or pronoun reference could mislead.
4. For user-facing output, return the clearer sentence rather than a long grammar note unless the user asks why.

Do not overcomplicate simple Georgian. The best syntax fix is often a shorter sentence with the same meaning.
